my name is minji. i spend my vacations above the treeline, with a heavy pack and no cell service.

i take type 2+ fun vacations on purpose. a week of cold hands, thin air, and route-finding mistakes does something a beach cannot: it makes the world simple again. eat, move, don't fall. everything else gets quiet.

"if you don't experience real physical fear from time to time, your mind will create fears out of nothing." an alex honnold idea i keep coming back to. without real stakes once in a while, we make mountains out of molehills. a small, managed dose of actual danger is the most reliable off switch i've found for a noisy mind.

last week i came down from seven days of alpine climbing in the bugaboos, a week of pure vertical movement. when you step off the wall, there is a specific relief: the dangerous part is over. an hour into the drive back to the airport, i witnessed a fatal car accident. it was the first time i had seen death that close. on a road with no signal for a 105 km stretch, people stood at the roadside trying iphone satellite sos to reach 911. all that technology, and we were helpless.

most people call the mountains the dangerous part and the drive home the safe part. the line between danger and safety is real, but it doesn't run where we draw it. spending my vacations in the places that make life feel most vivid is the right way for me.

it started with trail running and ultralight backpacking. then i became a patagonia brand ambassador, learned the other athletes' sports alongside them, and everything horizontal became vertical. my favorite genre is multi-pitch trad: the most moving parts in climbing, stacked hundreds of meters off the ground. in the bugaboos we met a couple who both climb 5.14; just bailing from mid-route had taken them 13 hours. grades don't protect you from the mountain's logistics. nothing else demands that much judgment, focus, and self-trust at once.

in winter i ski in the backcountry, avalanche news and all. i'm even building a tool that maps where powder is most likely to hold on a given slope. one foot in front of the other to the summit, an untouched run down: the best reset i know. i don't know how long this will last. for now, it's essential.

the mountains also gave me my people: rope partners, strangers sharing a hut at midnight, friends who read the same avalanche forecast before agreeing to go. nobody stays chill in a stormy alpine whiteout by accident. that's what i look for everywhere: people who meet ambiguity with a plan and a sense of humor. if that sounds like you, email me. i'm always looking for partners for the next adventure.
